Coca Cola

Commercials over the Years

  1. 1950

    In the 1950s, Coca-Cola was one of the most popular soft drinks in the United States, and the company used a variety of advertising techniques to promote its brand. One of the most memorable campaigns of the era was the "Pause That Refreshes" campaign, which encouraged people to take a break and enjoy a cold Coca-Cola. This campaign was highly successful and helped solidify Coca-Cola's place as a refreshing drink that was perfect for any occasion. Coca-Cola also utilized a lot of celebrity endorsements during this time, with famous actors and athletes like Marilyn Monroe and Joe DiMaggio appearing in advertisements for the drink. These advertisements often showed these celebrities enjoying a cold Coca-Cola, which helped create a sense of glamour and sophistication around the brand. Overall, Coca-Cola's advertising in the 1950s focused on creating a sense of happiness, relaxation, and refreshment associated with the drink, and the brand's continued success is a testament to the effectiveness of these techniques.

  2. 1960

    The "Things Go Better with Coke" campaign was a highly successful advertising campaign launched by Coca-Cola in the 1960s. The campaign's slogan was "Things Go Better with Coke," and it was designed to create a sense of joy, happiness, and fun around the brand. The "Things Go Better with Coke" campaign was highly successful, and it helped to solidify Coca-Cola's position as a fun and refreshing drink that was perfect for any occasion. The campaign's slogan became a cultural phenomenon, and it has been referenced in popular music, movies, and television shows over the years. Overall, the "Things Go Better with Coke" campaign was a defining moment in Coca-Cola's advertising history, and it helped to create a strong emotional connection between the brand and its consumers.

  3. 1970

    The "I'd Like to Buy the World a Coke" campaign was a highly successful advertising campaign launched by Coca-Cola in 1971. The campaign was designed to promote Coca-Cola as a drink that could bring people together and create a sense of unity and harmony around the world. The centerpiece of the campaign was a television commercial featuring a group of young people from around the world singing a song of the same name. The lyrics of the song celebrated the idea of peace, love, and understanding, and the commercial showed people of different races and nationalities coming together and sharing a Coke. The "I'd Like to Buy the World a Coke" campaign was highly successful, and it helped to create a strong emotional connection between the Coca-Cola brand and its consumers. The commercial became a cultural phenomenon, and the song was later re-recorded and released as a single, becoming a hit on the pop charts. Overall, the "I'd Like to Buy the World a Coke" campaign is considered one of the most successful advertising campaigns of all time, and it helped to solidify Coca-Cola's position as a brand that was not just selling a product, but was promoting a message of unity and harmony around the world.

  4. 1980

    The "Have a Coke and a Smile" campaign was a memorable advertising campaign launched by Coca-Cola in the 1980s. The campaign was designed to promote Coca-Cola as a source of happiness and joy in everyday life, and to reinforce the brand's emotional connection with consumers. The "Have a Coke and a Smile" campaign featured a series of television commercials that showed people from all walks of life enjoying a Coca-Cola and smiling. The commercials were accompanied by a catchy jingle that reinforced the message that Coca-Cola was all about happiness and joy. Overall, the "Have a Coke and a Smile" campaign is remembered as one of the most iconic and memorable advertising campaigns of all time. It is a testament to Coca-Cola's ongoing efforts to use creative and innovative advertising techniques to promote its brand and connect with consumers.

  5. 1990

    The "Coca-Cola Break" campaign was launched in 1991, and it featured a series of commercials and advertisements that showed people taking a break from their daily routine to enjoy a Coca-Cola. The commercials featuring Matt LeBlanc showed him enjoying a Coca-Cola at a bus stop, and the ad suggested that taking a break with a refreshing Coke was the perfect way to brighten up your day. One of the most memorable aspects of the campaign was a series of bus stop advertisements that featured a cut-out of Matt LeBlanc holding a Coke bottle. People could stand behind the cut-out and take a photo that made it look like they were standing next to the famous actor. The "Coca-Cola Break" campaign with Matt LeBlanc was highly successful, and it helped to reinforce Coca-Cola's position as a brand that was all about fun, relaxation, and enjoyment. The campaign also helped to introduce a new generation of consumers to the iconic Coca-Cola brand.

  6. 2000

    In 2000, Coca-Cola launched a new advertising campaign called "Enjoy." The campaign was designed to promote the idea that drinking a Coca-Cola was a simple and enjoyable way to enhance everyday experiences. The "Enjoy" campaign featured a series of television commercials that showed people enjoying a Coca-Cola in different situations, such as a picnic in the park or a night out with friends. The commercials were accompanied by a catchy jingle that reinforced the message that drinking a Coca-Cola was all about having fun and enjoying life. The "Enjoy" campaign was highly successful and helped to reinforce Coca-Cola's position as a brand that was all about fun, relaxation, and enjoyment. The campaign also helped to introduce a new generation of consumers to the iconic Coca-Cola brand.

  7. 2010

    The 2010 Coca Cola polar bear commercial, titled "The Happiness Factory: Snow Globes," was a hit during its debut at the Super Bowl. The commercial featured a family of animated polar bears enjoying Coca Cola in a snowy environment. The polar bears are depicted as playful and friendly, sharing Coca Cola with each other and having a good time. The commercial was created by the advertising agency Wieden+Kennedy and was part of Coca Cola's larger "Open Happiness" campaign, which aimed to connect the brand with positive emotions and experiences. The commercial was notable for its use of 3D animation, which allowed the polar bears to appear lifelike and interact with the environment in a believable way. The commercial was also praised for its family-friendly message and the way it conveyed the idea of Coca Cola as a beverage that brings people together and creates happiness. Since its debut in 1993, the polar bears have become a popular symbol for Coca Cola, appearing in numerous advertisements and even becoming the subject of their own animated short films. The company has also used its marketing power to raise awareness about polar bear conservation and to support efforts to protect their habitats

  8. 2020

    Coca Cola has a long history of creating memorable Christmas commercials, which have become a beloved part of the holiday season for many people. The company's first Christmas commercial aired in 1931 and featured a drawing of Santa Claus holding a bottle of Coca Cola. The commercial was well-received, and over the years, Coca Cola has continued to create Christmas advertisements that have become cultural touchstones. Coca Cola's Christmas commercials are known for their festive and nostalgic feel, often featuring classic holiday songs, colorful imagery, and heartwarming stories. The commercials have become a part of the brand's larger holiday marketing campaigns, which aim to spread joy and happiness during the Christmas season. The modern depiction of Santa Claus, as a jolly, rotund figure dressed in a red and white suit, is often attributed to Coca Cola's advertising campaigns. However, this is a myth. The image of Santa Claus as we know it today actually evolved over time from various cultural traditions and legends, including Saint Nicholas, a fourth-century Christian saint known for his generosity. While Coca Cola's advertising campaigns did help to popularize the modern image of Santa Claus, they did not invent the character. The company's use of Santa Claus in their advertisements was simply one of many cultural references that contributed to the development of the modern Santa Claus.
